Tax-exempt body entertainment fringe benefits

[3-3835] 
Summary
Non-deductible entertainment expenditure provided to an employee by an income tax-exempt employer constitutes a separate category of fringe benefit, a "tax-exempt body entertainment fringe benefit".
An employer is a "tax-exempt body" if the employer's income is either:
  • wholly exempt from income tax (eg a local government body, or a club which earns income from members only); or
  • partially exempt from income tax (eg a club that earns income from both members and non-members).
The taxable value is the amount of the entertainment expenditure, to the extent that the expenditure is incurred for the benefit of the employee.
In most circumstances, a tax-exempt body entertainment fringe benefit will not be able to qualify as a minor benefit.
[3-3840] 
Provision of entertainment
Taxation Determination TD 94/55 was released in an attempt to overcome the difficulties associated with ascertaining whether the provision of property contributes to the provision of entertainment. The determination suggests that regard should be taken of all the circumstances of the case.
Factors that should be taken into consideration include:
  • Timeliness:
    –
    the entertainment occurs soon after the provision of the item of property; or
    –
    the usefulness of the item of property expires after consumption; or
    –
    the item of property is returned at the completion of use.
  • Direct connection:
    –
    the entertainment arises from the use of the property; or
    –
    the entertainment is the expected outcome of the provision of the property.
Where items of property provided have an enduring character with only an indirect relationship to any immediate entertainment then these items of property generally don't constitute the provision of entertainment. Items such as bottled spirits, groceries, games, electrical appliances, household goods and swimming pools would not be considered entertainment, as the consumption is usually delayed and a further step is taken before the property is consumed.
However, if an item of property has a "dynamic and immediate" character, and consumption can occur immediately, then the provision will constitute entertainment. Examples may include theatre tickets, hot meals, glasses of champagne, holiday accommodation and hired entertainers.
Entertainment benefits are not classified as a specific type of fringe benefit, with the classification dependent on the circumstances in which the entertainment is provided. Accordingly, the provision of entertainment may result in the following fringe benefits:
  • expense payment benefit;
  • property benefit;
  • residual benefit;
  • airline transport benefit;
  • board benefit.
However, an employer has the option to elect that entertainment by way of food and drink is to be valued under the meal entertainment provisions (Div 9A). Refer to [3-3850].
[3-3850] 
Provision of food and drink as entertainment
The ATO has released Taxation Rulings TR 97/17 and TR 97/17A in an attempt to clarify the concept of what is entertainment as it relates to the provision of food and drink.
In order to determine whether entertainment has been provided, an objective analysis of all the circumstances surrounding the provision of the food and drink is required. In making this analysis an employer should consider:
  • what type of food and drink is being provided;
  • when is it being provided;
  • where it is being provided; and
  • why it is being provided.
Employers have the option of valuing some forms of entertainment as meal entertainment fringe benefits. Meal entertainment fringe benefits are fringe benefits provided by way of (or in connection with) food or drink. Note that this category does not include entertainment provided by way of recreation.
The taxable value of a meal entertainment fringe benefit can be calculated by one of the following methods where an election is made:
  • 50/50 split method: taxable value is equal to 50% of the expenditure incurred in providing meal entertainment to employees, their associates and non-employees. Under this method, 50% of the meal entertainment expenses is tax deductible and subject to FBT, while the remaining 50% is not tax deductible and not subject to FBT.
  • 12-week register method: taxable value of meal entertainment fringe benefits is calculated with reference to the percentage proportion provided as a fringe benefit to the total meal entertainment expenditure over the period. The taxable value is then calculated by multiplying the register percentage by the total meal entertainment expenses during the FBT year.
    The register must be kept for a continuous period of 12 weeks and can be relied upon for five FBT years, or until total expenditure varies by more than 20%.
Note: Where an employer does not elect either of the above methods, the taxable value will be the actual amount of expenditure incurred in providing the relevant fringe benefit.
